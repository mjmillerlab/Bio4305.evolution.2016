What is evolution, and why study evolution?

Evolution is the phenomenon of inherited change in biological organisms over time. Scientists repeatedly and ubiquitously observe organismal change overtime, and more recently have linked this change to the principle molecular driver of inheritance (the genetic code). Thus the field of evolutionary biology seeks to understand what are the processes that drive inherited change, which include studying both how the observable organism (i.e. the phenotype), as well as the genotype, changes over successive generations.

Evolution touches on some of the most fundamental questions of our being. And therefore, in mainstream society, the field of evolutionary biology is among the most controversial, misrepresented, and polemical sub-discipline in biology. Most biologists consider mastery of basic evolution as indispensable for any higher-level understanding in biology, and Dobzhansky’s famous quote: “nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution,” is more true now in the age of genomes than perhaps Dobzhansky could have imagined in 1973.

I realize that very few of you will become professional evolutionary biologists, but whether you become a medical professional, a biomedical researcher, a teacher, a forensic detective, or a zookeeper, I hope that when you think about biology, you will think like an evolutionary biologist. (borrowed from J. McDonald, U. Delaware).

Course Outline

The course is comprised of three-days-per-week lecture section and once-per-week laboratory section. Lectures are divided into three roughly equal sections. The first addresses macroevolutionary patterns in the history of life. The second section addresses the genetic basis of evolutionary change and adaptive evolution. The third section addresses sexual selection, parental and genome conflicts, human evolution, and evolutionary medicine.

My Expectations

If you read the assignments BEFORE class, engage during lecture and lab discussion sessions (that means talking), complete the writing assignments and most importantly: DEMONSTRATE that you have thought critically about the material that you are presented, you will probably find that this course is relatively easy. I am working very hard to develop and adapt a course that teaches the fundamentals of evolutionary biology in a manner that is most relevant to you. On the other hand, if you don’t do the readings, disengage during discussions and lectures, and cram just before exams, you may consider this course to be extremely brutal.

Specifically, a substantial portion of your grade will come from in-lecture reading quizzes, which will occur at the beginning of most morning lectures. The questions won’t be hard, and there won’t be gotcha questions; the point is two-fold: the quizzes will establish your attendance, and they keep us all honest about the reading. Above, I stressed that my job is to take the fundamental material and make it as relevant as possible. By establishing before lecture that we’ve all read and grasped at least most of the basics, I can focus my time in lecture on detailed review of the any topics that are conceptually tricky and embellish the fundamentals with the most engaging and relevant real-world examples. There is NO make-up for missed reading quizzes. If you have an excused absence for a given reading quiz, your reading quiz grade is the average of all other quizzes.

Course Textbook

Zimmer, C., & D.J. Emlen. Evolution: Making Sense of Life. 2nd Edition. Roberts & Company, 2015 Amazon link

About the text book....it’s awesome. And it’s a real quantum improvement in how evolutionary biology is presented to undergraduates. It’s written by Carl Zimmer, a New York Times science writer, and Doug Emlen, who’s a great active evolutionary biologist. I think that you’ll find that Zimmer’s experience framing biology to the educated public — he’s one of the best science writers on the planet — makes the material much more accessible and engaging.

The textbook is really heavy. Please don’t bring the book to class, you might throw out your back. Read it at home BEFORE class. From a pedagogical standpoint, I recommend buying rather than renting the book, so that you can liberally mark the margins. It’s been shown repeatedly that such annotations improve learning. I’m not sure if we know how that extends to digital annotations.

Recommended: Pechenik, J.A. A Short Guide to Writing About Biology. Pearson Longmans, 2012–2016. (7th, 8th, or 9th editions acceptable) Amazon link

You are responsible for mastering the material in the writing guide. However, how you access it is up to you. Amazon has this book listed at about 55 dollars. That is too much; 20-30 dollars is appropriate, and you might find very good deals on earlier versions of this book. If you intend to write a thesis in Biology, or go on to graduate school, you might as well buy the a copy now (you will wear the pages thin). If not, perhaps you will consider buying a copy with a friend. Students expecting to get a high grade in Evolution need to follow the writing canon that Pechenik articulates in his book.

Laboratory Discussion

Each week in lab you will engage with primary scientific literature. I expect that you will have read carefully the article several times, and that you have gone beyond the article to understand the methods, results, figures, and take-home message of the paper. To help you be accountable for the reading, there will be a lab reading quiz each lab session for which there is a primary reading. We will discuss the paper in a modified journal club style, which means that our class will be divided into two smaller sections, and you should expect to be called and discuss any aspect of the paper during the discussion. This can include going to the board and explaining what is going on in a figure.

Reading primary literature is quite different than reading the textbook, and requires a much different level of engagement. Even experienced senior academic scientists need to read most primary literature papers several times, and have to look up terms and techniques. Do not plan on lightly skimming the paper and hoping that lab discussion goes well….it won’t.

Viewpoint Assignment

The Biology department has identified Evolution as a both a writing-enriched and a Research Requirement course. The goal of the Research requirement is to engage students in the critical skills associated with academic research. Therefore, each student will be assigned a topic that is contemporarily relevant in evolutionary biology. The idea is to take themes that are relevant to society at large, but that touch on evolution. Imagine a sophisticated cocktail party. However, you will engage the topic as a formal academic researcher, and you will review and synthesize the state of our knowledge on the topics. You will present this synthesis in the form of a standard term paper (which practicing scientists call review papers) In this class, the review paper will be short format, and focused around 1–3 recent scientific article(s). Thus it is best defined as a viewpoint article.

As is required of a writing enhanced paper, you will write a suitable first draft, which will be peer-reviewed by a classmate. You will revise your paper, and submit it to me for revision. Finally, I will give you feedback on how to improve the paper for a final draft. You will also present your synthesis to the class during lecture as a 12 minute presentation. Again, this is a standard format at professional conferences, with 3 minutes for questions from the class.

You will follow the PLOS style guidelines. However, as a viewpoint, you are limited to 1500 words and 15 citations. Your topic paper will be formatted as a pre-submission inquiry. Specifically, they will outline the focus of the viewpoint in 500 words accompanied by a referenced abstract (not included in word count).

Here is an example of a viewpoint that I published in 2015. And here is the pre-submission inquiry. Obviously, you won't have methodology or key results. But you should have background and conclusions.

You will also present your viewpoint to the class as an oral presentation (with slideshow). You will need to submit your presentation file (as Powerpoint, Keynote, Prezi, or PDF) via Dropbox by 2 PM on the day of your presentation. Presentations will follow standard scientific format in that they are 12 minutes with 3 minutes allowed for questions. Students will be graded both on their presentation as well as the quality of their questions during other students' presentations.
